## Hard invariants (the header block, every scene inherits these; none may break them)

1. **ONE controlling morph.** Free field to the **duel web** (`duelweb`, C9-2), plus its free reverse leg (C9-9). That is the chapter's entire morph budget. Nothing else re-sorts the field.
2. **Four held scalars over the morph, no second re-sort.** `duelReveal` (0 free to 1 web, the morph itself), `duelDominance` (0 identity color to 1 the red/blue "who came out on top" hue), `duelDustDim` (a luminance multiplier that sinks the 236,821 dust balls so the strands pop), and `strandRecede` (0 all strands at full chroma to 1 only the in-focus strands carry chroma while the rest fall to a faint neutral gray, so the argued rivalries pop out of the crossing-line tangle). All are lerps over the held layout, NOT morphs, and all default inert (`strandRecede` 0) so every prior release renders byte-identically.
3. **The 2D annotation-plane scenes.** The auction heartbeat (C9-4) and the loyalty spectrum (C9-6) are DOM/SVG telemetry over the held (dimmed) web. The player-to-player strands, the player-node dots, the tap-a-duel replay strip (C9-3, C9-8), and the season scrub across the flatline years (C9-5) are all scene-authored SVG over the held web. The payoff (C9-8) is DOM.
4. **Payload budget.** `scenes/ch9.json` plus its inline 16 payoff cards plus the one new per-point buffer `pairing.u16` (about **93 KB gz**) ship well inside the **2MB-per-chapter gzip budget**.
5. **The attribute ceiling holds at 14 (release-blocking-safe).** The pairing (which duel each ball belongs to) is delivered as a **data texture `uPairingTex` indexed by point-index in-shader**, NOT as a 15th vertex attribute. The field STAYS at 14 attributes. A 15th attribute blacked out the whole field on one build (per the field integration map and CONTRACT §24.2), so this is not optional. **This OVERRIDES the stale CONTRACT §26 RESERVED stub, which still describes a 15th-attribute `aPairing` plan; §6a rewrites that stub.**
6. **Reduced motion = live end-state jump-cuts.** Every scene renders its landed state instantly, no motion or tap required. The morph forces t=1; the dominance hue, the dust dim, the heartbeat EKG, the loyalty spectrum, the season scrub, and the replay strip render as static end states.
7. **Glossary rule.** No statistical term of art appears in main-flow or teaser copy. Every device carries a fan-language stage name on screen; the technical name lives one click deep in the footnote (§0.2). It is "who came out on top," never "empirical-Bayes dominance"; "the same players kept meeting," never "temporal graph"; "how much of the squad came back," never "Jaccard."
8. **The WPL framing rule.** The WPL is never "behind." In Chapter 9 it is **a young league whose fabric is forming fast**: its rivalry web is knotting together at the same age the men's game did, and it has its own auction pulse. Banned as an object of "behind / lags / catching up / years behind / immature."
9. **One hue-meaning at a time (the red/blue is dominance only).** Red and blue mean ONE thing in this chapter: who came out on top of a duel (red = the batter, blue = the bowler). Era is NEVER a second red/blue ramp; era is carried by **position** (the layout drifts the 2008 crowd to one edge and today's stars to the other). The dust is a dim neutral scatter, never colored, and QA must confirm the PRODUCTION dust is truly neutral (the layout-debug preview renders it era-tinted; that tint ships nowhere). Red/blue (not red/green) is the deliberate colorblind-safe choice for the dominance channel, but the actual production hexes have never been CVD-simulated (the preview is era-colored, not dominance-colored): **before build, run a protan/deutan/tritan simulation on the real red pole, the real blue pole, and the neutral dust, and confirm a LIGHTNESS delta between red and blue and from the dust**, so the load-bearing read never rides on hue alone. Because the dominance value is shrunk toward the mean, most strands land PALE (near-even duels), so saturation reads as how lopsided a duel was and a pale strand means it was even; on-screen copy must never imply a hard red-or-blue binary.
10. **Persistence through churn (the integrity spine).** The chapter's whole claim is a contrast, not a cause: institutions (squads, careers) churn on a clock, and the human rivalries persist through it. It never claims a duel "caused" anything, never claims the mega-auction "makes" rivalries stronger, and never claims loyalty "protects" a team. It shows what churns and what stays, side by side.
11. **Artifact wins.** Every {braced} literal and every on-screen number traces to a `scenes/ch9.json` field; the emitted value wins over any teaser here if rounding or definition differs.

### 0.1 Encoding grammar, the duel web, the dominance hue, the dust, the auction heartbeat, the loyalty spectrum (cognitive-design)

- **The unit is unchanged, a dot is still one ball, and the reader watches balls fly to their rivalry (the controlling morph).** In `free`, the field is 316,199 balls scattered in the sky. In `duelweb`, every ball that belongs to a real rivalry flies to that rivalry's strand; every other ball sinks into a dim background dust. So the reader keeps the "a dot is a ball" contract from Chapter 1 and learns two new marks on top of it: **a strand is a duel** and **the dust is everything else**. The morph is the constellation / match-dots precedent (in-shader from a data texture, no positions cross the wire, §6a). The web is taught in C9-2 before any argument is made on it, and the new mark is taught on ONE clean isolated strand (Kohli-Jadeja pulled out of the sky and named) BEFORE the full web resolves around it (C9-2 step 2), so the reader learns the mark on a legible instance, not on the hairball (worked-example-before-complexity).

- **A strand is a duel: two players who faced each other at least 30 balls, and there are 1,691 of them.** A duel is a batter-and-bowler pairing that met at least thirty legal balls across the whole history. The layout places the 277 players (244 men, 33 women) as hubs and draws a strand between any two who form a duel; the busiest players (Kohli with 76 duels, Dhawan 65, Jadeja 59, Rohit 57) sit dead centre because everyone has a rivalry with them. The reader is taught "a strand is a duel between two players" before any strand is argued on, because it is the easiest new mark to misread as "a strand is a team" or "a strand is a season."

- **About a quarter of every ball ever bowled lives in a rivalry; three quarters is dust (the field's own honest split).** Of the 316,199 balls, **79,378 (25.1%)** land in one of the 1,691 duels and **236,821 (74.9%)** stay as background dust. The dust is not decoration and not deleted: it is the three quarters of cricket that never became a decade-long rivalry, drawn as a dim neutral scatter so the bright strands read as the exception. `duelDustDim` sinks the dust luminance so the load-bearing "the rivalries are the story, the rest is dust" read is a **brightness** contrast (bright strands over dim dust), which is colorblind-robust, before any color is added. The dust dim ramps CONCURRENTLY with the fly (it begins early, at p ≈ 0.1, not late), so the salient motion the reader sees is balls-organizing-onto-strands, not balls-scattering-away (common fate / object constancy): the 25% resolving into bright rivalries out-reads the 75% sinking into the haze.

- **The strand color is who came out on top, red for the batter, blue for the bowler (the dominance hue).** Each duel's color is a single value clamped to plus-or-minus one: **+1 batter-red** (the batter scored freely and got out rarely across the years), **minus-1 bowler-blue** (the bowler kept the runs down and took the wickets). It is the empirical-Bayes-shrunk runs-per-ball for the pair against the league mean, so a 30-ball duel does not out-shout a 160-ball one. `duelDominance` lerps the strands and their in-strand balls from identity color to the red/blue hue (the heatColor / rivers gated-hue precedent; hue stays identity for every prior scene). Red-versus-blue is chosen deliberately over red-versus-green so the "who won" read survives red-green colorblindness (about 8% of a male-skewed audience), and the production red/blue/dust hexes must pass a protan/deutan/tritan lightness check before build (invariant 9). Because the value is shrunk, most strands land PALE (near-even duels), so **saturation carries how lopsided a duel was and a pale strand means it was even**; the copy teaches this ("the more one-sided, the deeper the color") so a faint tint is never over-read as a clean win, and the arbitrary color binding gets a verbal hook, **red = runs (the batter)**, so the legend is recalled, not re-learned. Era is NOT a second red/blue ramp (invariant 9); era is position.

- **The strands are receded to make the argued rivalry findable (`strandRecede`, the anti-hairball scalar).** The 1,691 strands are scene-authored SVG lines hub-to-hub, and all of them draw at once; with about forty mutually-connected stars the core alone carries several hundred crossing lines, which `duelDustDim` (a scalar on the 316k balls, not on the lines) never touches. So a second focus scalar, `strandRecede`, lerps every NON-focus strand toward a faint neutral gray (and drops its in-strand knot to neutral, low alpha) while the in-focus strands keep full red/blue chroma, and every strand's base opacity is weighted by its ball count so light duels fade first. This yields three strand tiers (C9-3): the **hero** duel (extra width, full chroma, a pulsing knot, and a pinned leader-line callout, so it pops out of the tangle by the one preattentive channel that survives clutter, motion, plus a label so it is never found by tracing a line through the crowd), the **long** duels (the 232 that ran 8+ seasons, full chroma, opacity-weighted), and **the rest** (receded to gray). Non-focus strands carry no chroma during a beat, so only the argued rivalries are colored, and the field's focus set is driven by `setDuelFocus` (§6a).

- **The season scrub is the thesis, and it is scene-authored SVG over the held web (C9-5).** Each strand knows the seasons the pair actually met (`duel.seasons`, `duel.span`). Scrubbing the season slider lights each strand only in the years its two players faced a ball and dims it otherwise, so the reader watches the web draw itself in year by year. The five mega-auction years are drawn as vertical reset markers that sweep across; the point the eye reads is that the strands do NOT break at a reset. The web positions are fixed throughout, so the scrub is a visibility-and-luminance change, never a re-sort or a second morph.

- **The auction heartbeat is a 2D EKG, and the flatlines are the data's own troughs (C9-4).** Squad overlap is drawn as a line per season, "how much of a team came back," on a labelled axis from 0 to 1: high means the band stayed together. The line rests around **0.46** most years and **crashes to about 0.19** in the five mega-auction years (2011, 2014, 2018, 2022, 2025), so the chart reads as a steady pulse with five synchronized flatlines. This is a 2D telemetry scene, NOT a field morph: the particles carry no meaning in an EKG, so nothing about the heartbeat is drawn with the field (the morph budget, invariant 1). The load-bearing "steady, then it crashes" read is a **position-on-axis** read, backed by the axis label, never a color read.

- **The loyalty spectrum is a 2D line with two named ends (C9-6).** One line, "one-club players," the share of players four seasons deep who only ever wore one franchise's shirt, falling from about **27 in 100 to about 12** across the seasons. At the far end, one annotated marker: the player who wore the most shirts, **Aaron Finch, 9**. One idea, one falling line, one extreme, so the churn-in-careers point never outruns the number.

- **One label for one concept.** A dot is "a ball." A strand is "a duel" (and the whole picture is "the duel web" or "the rivalry web") after C9-2. The dust is "the dust." Red is "the batter came out on top," blue is "the bowler did." The heartbeat line is "how much of the squad came back"; a mega-auction trough is "a flatline" or "a reset." A one-club player is "a one-club player" (never "a loyalist" bare on screen without the gloss). The WPL is "the young league whose fabric is forming fast," never "behind."

### 0.2 Register, PERSISTENCE THROUGH CHURN (on-screen copy rules, Ch 9)

The chapter's voice is **elegiac but warm, and celebratory**. It is the companion to Chapter 2's elegy, which mourned the anchor as a dying craft; Chapter 9 mourns the churn of institutions (squads torn up, careers scattered) but then turns and celebrates the thing that survives it (the rivalries, the human fabric). Chapter 2 said "look what we are losing"; Chapter 9 says "look what refuses to go." Five guardrails, on screen in every scene:

1. **Show the contrast, never assert a cause (the integrity spine, release-blocking).** The chapter says "institutions churn AND the rivalries persist," two facts held side by side. It never says the mega-auction "makes" rivalries stronger, never says a long duel "kept" a player at a club, never says loyalty "wins." "Causes / because of / makes / protects / drives" as causal assertions are banned in main flow; the honest verbs are "churns / holds / survives / re-draws / keeps meeting / refuses to move."

2. **The WPL is a young league whose fabric is forming fast, never behind (the house WPL framing rule, made literal).** C9-7 shows the WPL's own rivalry web knotting together at the same age the men's game did and its own auction pulse (a first big reset in 2026, overlap down to about 26 in 100). That is framed as **a league building its fabric fast**, never as one catching up. "Behind / catching up / years behind / developing toward / immature / a different animal" are banned; the WPL is "young," "forming fast," "already building rivalries that will run for a decade."

3. **Orient every exhibit before you argue with it.** Nobody is asked to read a web, a glowing strand, an EKG, a falling line, or a season scrub until the mark has been taught in dead-plain words: what a dot, a strand, and the dust are (C9-2), what red and blue mean (C9-2), what a long strand is (C9-3), what the heartbeat line and a flatline are (C9-4), what a one-club player is (C9-6). The duel web gets the most patient orientation because "a strand is a duel between two people" is the easiest new mark to misread.

4. **Interaction adds depth, never carries the thesis.** Tapping a strand for its ball-by-ball replay (C9-3, C9-8), scrubbing the season slider (C9-5), and tapping a franchise on the heartbeat (C9-4) are all optional depth. The scripted scroll lands every point without a tap, and reduced motion carries every beat.

5. **One number per idea; the extras go to the footnote.** Each caption step carries one moving number (a before-and-after pair counts as one idea). Full per-season heartbeat ladders, the loyalty series, the shrinkage constants, the ≥30-ball sensitivity, and the Collapse Contagion figure live in the footnotes, never on the main-flow card.

## 12. The demoted-exhibit footnotes, in full (the one the beat budget pushed off the main flow)

### `ch9-collapse`, Collapse Contagion (served from C9-3)

**The contrarian companion to Chapter 8's momentum, kept as a footnote.** Commentary loves the idea of a collapse: one wicket brings the next, and suddenly a team is three down for nothing. We tested it the same way Chapter 8 tested a wicket bringing a wicket, by asking whether a wicket really makes the next delivery more dangerous than the same balls dealt back in a random order. It does not. The aftershock read comes out at about 0.94, below one, which means a wicket makes the very next ball slightly LESS likely to be another wicket, not more. That lines up with Chapter 8's finding that a wicket after a wicket runs about 0.93 and never clears shuffled cricket in the modern game. So the collapse is mostly a story we tell after the fact: the new batter, if anything, gets a moment of quiet, not a hail of wickets. We keep this off the main-flow web because Chapter 9 is about what persists, not about the momentum audit, but it is the honest aftershock companion to the wicket belief Chapter 8 already graded. (The technical names, the self-exciting aftershock read and the shuffle test, live here.)
