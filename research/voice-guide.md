# Every Ball Ever — Voice & Copy Guide

The single reference for every word a reader sees: scene captions, labels, tooltips, payoff cards, buttons, the end card, footnotes. Applies to all shipped scenes and every chapter from here on.

## Who is reading, and how

Cricket fans and cricket nerds. Smart about the game, not about statistics. Reading on a phone, one thumb, half-distracted, mid-scroll. If a caption does not land on the first read in under ten seconds, it has failed.

**The test for every caption:** show it to someone who just landed on that screen. Can they say, in one breath, what they are looking at and what the point is? If not, rewrite.

## The six rules

**1. Orient, then reveal.** The first sentence says plainly what is on the screen before it makes any point. What are the dots? What do the axes mean? What does a color mean? Only then the insight. Never make an argument about a picture the reader cannot yet read. This is the fix for "I have no idea what I'm looking at."

**2. No em dashes. Ever.** Use a period or a comma. Two short sentences beat one long one with a dash in the middle. (The current copy has 323 of them. Zero is the target.)

**3. Say the plain thing.** No "a different animal entirely." No "not X, not Y." No "the sighter, visible from space." Drop the flourish and state it. A vivid plain sentence beats a clever vague one.

**4. One idea per caption. One number per idea.** If a caption carries three numbers, keep the one that lands the point and move the rest to the footnote. Lead with the insight, not the data.

**5. Plain words for cricket-stats jargon.** If a technical term must appear, show what it means in the same breath.

| Instead of | Say |
|---|---|
| dismissal / dismissal hazard | gets out / the odds of getting out |
| the out-rate | how often batters get out |
| boundary-light accumulator | a batter who nudges singles instead of hitting big |
| strike rate 135 | scoring 135 runs per 100 balls (or "scoring fast") |
| RPO / runs per over | runs an over |
| the going rate / par | the score a good team would be on |
| economy | runs a bowler leaks per over |
| anchor | a batter who bats slow and stays in |
| powerplay | the first six overs, when fielders are pushed up |

**6. Talk to the reader as "you," with a bit of swagger.** The voice is a mate on the couch who really knows cricket, leaning over to show you something class. Warm, confident, a little funny. Clarity always comes first: the orientation sentence stays dead plain. Then the point can have some edge and character. Never like a report or a textbook, but never forced-jokey either. If a line would make a real fan smile and nod, it is working. If it would make them wince, it is trying too hard.

**Footnotes get the same clear voice.** The "how we computed this" panels are for the nerds who click in, so they can go deeper. They can name a stat term, but always say what it means in plain words right there. Deep does not mean dense.

## Orient-then-reveal, shown

A scene usually gets two or three caption steps. Spend the first purely on orientation. Make the point in the next.

- **Step 1 (orient):** "Every dot is one ball ever bowled. The further right, the more balls the batter had faced. The higher up, the more runs they had."
- **Step 2 (reveal):** "A fast innings climbs steeply. A slow one stays flat."

## Before and after

**Cold open, the reveal**
- Before: "You were 58 two-hundreds too gentle about 2026. The real number: 65. For fifteen years you'd have been right — then the ceiling broke."
- After: "You guessed low. In 2026, teams passed 200 sixty-five times. For the first fifteen years your line would've been close. Then scoring took off."

**Chapter 1, the ignition wall**
- Before: "Every ball, arranged by how long its batter had been in. Left edge: a batter's first balls. Rows climb from 2008 at the bottom to 2026 at the top. Brightness = runs."
- After: "Here is every ball again. Now each one sits by how long the batter had been in. The left edge is their very first balls. Old seasons sit at the bottom, new ones at the top. Brighter means more runs."

**Chapter 2, the worm field**
- Before: "Draw an innings as a rising line: runs climbing with every ball faced. Steep line, fast scoring. Flat line, slow. Every ball ever bowled is in the haze."
- After: "Every dot is one ball. The further right, the more balls the batter had faced. The higher up, the more runs. A fast innings climbs steeply. A slow one stays flat. Every ball ever bowled is in this cloud."

**Chapter 2, the anchor** (calibrated to the "mate on the couch" voice)
- Before: "And this is an anchor. Same runs to chase, a different animal entirely — patient, boundary-light, holding on."
- After: "The gold line is one anchor's innings. Same target, but look at it crawl. Barely a big shot in sight. Just knock a single, live to face the next one, and let the big hitters swing at the other end."

**The dial, calibrated:** orientation stays plain ("The gold line is one anchor's innings"). The point gets the character ("look at it crawl," "let the big hitters swing"). That is the mix everywhere: clear setup, then a line with some life in it.

## What stays

Keep the numbers exactly as the data gives them (they are data-bound, never hand-typed). Keep the chapter's feeling: Chapter 2 still mourns the anchor. Voice does not mean flat. It means clear first, then felt.
